"What will this project ask of me?"

The wondrous process of cell division is churning in billions of your very own cells. As you read on, chromosomes are condensing. Spindle fibers are assembling. Cells are preparing to pull themselves apart with extraordinary precision, then seal two new membranes around two identical sets of chromosomes, and carry on. For this project, we don't want you to merely memorize this sequence. Rather, we want you to understand it well enough to translate it into movement, sound, image, or narrative. You'll explain in a way that enables someone who has never thought about mitosis (like the person next to you, probably) to follow what is happening, and, hopefully, feel something of the drama of the process.

That translation is the test. A diagram copied from a textbook proves you can copy. A dance that makes an audience feel the tension of chromosomes held at the metaphase plate... Well, that proves you understand the tension.

The core requirement: Your finished work must represent the sequence and key events of cell division accurately enough that an uninformed audience could follow them. Your process note, completed in the Telophase section below, will explain every significant choice you made and why.

"How should I show each phase?"

In order, with accuracy. The sub-phases of Interphase (G1, S, and G2) may be represented as a single preparatory state.

Interphase
Cell grows; DNA is fully replicated in preparation for division
Prophase
Chromatin condenses; spindle begins to form
Prometaphase
Nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle attaches
Metaphase
Chromosomes align at the cell's equator
Anaphase
Sister chromatids pulled to opposite poles
Telophase
New nuclei form; chromosomes relax
Cytokinesis
Cytoplasm divides; two daughter cells emerge

Understanding Cell Division

The resources below are vetted, citable, and built for serious inquiry.

Under a fluorescence microscope, a cell entering prophase is genuinely startling. The chromosomes — each one a pair of sister chromatids joined at a structure called the centromere — become visible as distinct, thickening threads. The spindle apparatus, built from protein filaments called microtubules, radiates outward from two organizing centers as the cell prepares for division. The nuclear envelope remains intact through prophase; it is in prometaphase, the phase immediately following, that it breaks down and the spindle fibers make contact with the chromosomes. It is molecular machinery operating at a scale far below the resolution of the naked eye — individual microtubules are roughly 25 nanometers across — with a fidelity that would embarrass any human-built mechanism.

Use the resources below to build an understanding of this process precise enough to represent it in your chosen medium. Note vocabulary as you read. Use the research prompts below to record what you're learning in your own words (not copied from a source). If you can explain a phase without looking, you are ready to represent it creatively.

Understanding Your Medium

Every medium has a history, a grammar, and ends it accomplishes that no other medium can.

The animators who first visualized DNA replication at the molecular level in films produced at the Walter and Eliza Hall Institute in the early 2000s faced choices that had never been faced before. What color should a microtubule be? How fast should the spindle appear to move, relative to human perception of time? What to leave out, and what would an audience need to see? These are not aesthetic but epistemic decisions, choices about what counts as a faithful representation of something no human eye has directly witnessed.

Your project will require the same kind of thinking. The resources below are organized by medium. Use them to research the tradition you are entering: its conventions, its possibilities, and the creative choices it demands of anyone who works in it.

Select your medium below. Then use the research prompts at the bottom of this section to plan how you will represent each stage of cell division within it. The goal is to think seriously about how your medium handles sequence, time, tension, and resolution before the making starts.

Zines are self-published, often hand-assembled works with roots in punk, feminist, and underground communities. Comics and sequential art add the dimension of narrative pacing. The reader controls time by moving through panels. Both formats make sequencing and visual language the central concern, which makes them unusually well-suited to a process with distinct, ordered stages. The gutter between panels is not just space, but an opportunity for collaboration as the reader performs the act of sequence.

Stop motion is among the oldest animation techniques: objects moved in tiny increments and photographed frame by frame, creating the illusion of movement from a sequence of stills. The medium rewards deliberate control over pacing and sequence. These qualities are central to representing a process as precisely staged as cell division. Every frame is a decision. Editing is, in a real sense, the animation itself.

Scientific illustration has its own long history, from the anatomical drawings of Vesalius in the sixteenth century to the fluorescence microscopy images that define how we picture cells today. Every image in that tradition involved interpretive choices: what to emphasize, what to simplify, what colors to assign to structures that have no natural color at the scale of observation. Think of composition, line weight, and color not as decoration but as argument — and as something that can be right or wrong about the science it depicts.

The Tradition

Representing science visually and creatively is a discipline with a long, serious history.

Over the course of the early 2000s, the molecular animator Drew Berry released a series of visualizations of DNA replication and cellular processes that had never been attempted at that level of scientific accuracy or aesthetic resolution. Working from published biochemistry at the Walter and Eliza Hall Institute in Melbourne — where he has been based since 1995 — Berry translated molecular data into three-dimensional animated sequences. He had to decide what color microtubules should be, as they have no intrinsic color at the scale of a nanometer. He had to decide how to represent the thermal noise that jostles every molecule in a cell, the constant random agitation that makes real biochemistry look nothing like the clean diagrams in textbooks. He had to decide what to leave out. His films went on to appear in museum installations, music videos, and a TED talk, and influenced how a generation of biologists pictures its own subject.

The work you are doing is in this tradition. The resources below will give you a sense of how scientists, animators, illustrators, and filmmakers have approached the challenge of making the invisible visible. What is at stake, scientifically and aesthetically, in every one of those choices?

A question to bring to every resource in this section: Where did the maker have to choose between accuracy and clarity? Between beauty and precision? How did they handle what could not be directly seen? These are the choices you will face in your project (and the choices your process note will need to address).

Every finished work carries the record of its making. The process note makes that record explicit.

The artist statement shouldn't just summarize your project. A summary describes what is there. A statement accounts for the thinking behind it: the choices made, the alternatives considered and rejected, the moments when the medium's limits and the content's demands didn't match, and how you handled those moments. A thoughtful account of a genuine difficulty is more valuable, as a piece of writing, than a polished description of something that went easily. The difficulty is where the thinking happened.
